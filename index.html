About the Book

"Mein Kampf" (German for "My Struggle") is a political and autobiographical book written by Adolf Hitler in 1924 while he was imprisoned after the failed Beer Hall Putsch in Germany.

The book combines Hitler’s life story with his political ideology. It outlines ideas that later became central to Nazi ideology.

Today the book is studied mainly in academic and historical contexts to understand propaganda, extremist ideology, and the political climate of early 20th-century Germany.

Author Background

Early Life

Adolf Hitler was born in Austria in 1889. He later moved to Germany and served as a soldier during World War I.

Political Rise

After World War I, he joined the German Workers’ Party, which later became the Nazi Party.

Writing the Book

Hitler wrote most of the book during his imprisonment in Landsberg Prison in 1924.

Historical Timeline

1919 – Hitler joins the German Workers' Party.

1923 – Beer Hall Putsch fails in Munich.

1924 – Hitler is imprisoned and begins writing "Mein Kampf".

1925 – First volume published.

1926 – Second volume published.

1933 – Hitler becomes Chancellor of Germany.

Key Ideas Discussed in the Book

  • Extreme nationalism
  • Authoritarian political leadership
  • Racial ideology and propaganda
  • Expansionist ideas for Germany

These ideas later influenced the policies of Nazi Germany and contributed to events that led to World War II.

Historical Criticism

Historians and scholars widely criticize the book for promoting hateful and extremist ideology. Many countries regulate its distribution or provide annotated editions to explain its historical context.

Today it is mainly studied in universities and history courses to help understand how propaganda and extremist beliefs can influence societies.
